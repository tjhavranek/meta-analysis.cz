Native and Immigrant Labor Substitution

»    a meta-analysis

Abstract

This paper presents the first comprehensive meta-analysis of the elasticity of substitution between native and immigrant labor. Drawing on 1,091 estimates from 41 studies, we examine the extent to which published estimates are shaped by methodological choices and publication selection bias. We find strong evidence of selective reporting: less precise estimates are systematically associated with lower reported elasticities. Correcting for these biases using a wide array of metaregression techniques raises the mean elasticity (sigma) from an uncorrected 13 to approximately 22. Model averaging techniques reveal that data features, specifically geographic scale, data granularity, and worker experience, explain much of the heterogeneity in reported results. While the choice between log(mean wage) and mean(log wage) remains a substantial driver of variation, its impact is moderated once publication bias and modeling scale are controlled for. Our "best practice" estimates, which prioritize the most granular data, suggest an elasticity of approximately 17 for regional models. While publication bias masks a higher potential elasticity, rigorous research designs that isolate pure substitution effects yield a stable benchmark that is more conservative than a simple bias correction yet higher than the uncorrected mean.
